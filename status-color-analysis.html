Carbon Design System, Material Design 3, Fluent 2 -- logic behind their choices and how our DS compares

2. Logic Behind Each System's Decisions

Carbon: Why Grade Shifting?

Carbon uses higher grades (darker) for light themes and lower grades (lighter) for dark themes. The logic: on a white background, you need a darker shade of red to hit 4.5:1 contrast. On a near-black background, a lighter shade provides the same contrast. This is why Error goes from Red 60 (dark red, 5.0:1 on white) to Red 50 (brighter red, 5.4:1 on #161616).

Special case: Warning stays Yellow 30 in ALL themes. Carbon treats warning yellow as an indicator color (icon or status dot), never as standalone text on a background. Yellow 30 on white is only 1.7:1 -- it fails even AA. Carbon always pairs it with a dark text label next to the icon, or places dark text on a tinted yellow container. This is fundamentally different from using warning as a button color.

Why separate Caution Major (Orange) from Warning (Yellow)? Carbon distinguishes severity levels: Yellow = minor caution (informational), Orange = major caution (action needed), Red = critical error. This gives them 3 levels of warm-spectrum urgency with increasing hue shift toward red.

M3: Why the Tonal Palette Approach?

M3's HCT system solves a fundamental problem: perceptual uniformity. In HSL, two colors at the same "lightness" value can look very different in perceived brightness (yellow at L=50 looks much brighter than blue at L=50). HCT's "Tone" dimension is calibrated to actual perceived luminance, so Tone 40 of any hue produces roughly the same apparent lightness.

Why Tone 40 for light, Tone 80 for dark? Tone 40 on a Tone 98 surface (light) gives approximately 7:1 contrast. Tone 80 on a Tone 6 surface (dark) gives approximately 7:1 contrast. The system is designed so that light and dark schemes achieve equivalent contrast automatically. The Container pattern (Tone 90 light / Tone 30 dark) provides a subtle tinted background for less prominent elements.

Why no native Warning/Success? M3 limits the baseline scheme to Primary, Secondary, Tertiary + Error. Warning/Success are considered application-specific and should be added as custom extended colors following the same tonal logic. This keeps the core system lean and avoids imposing specific semantic meanings on colors that may vary by product domain.

Fluent 2: Why Named Ramps with Cranberry?

Fluent uses named color ramps (Cranberry, not "Red") to decouple the semantic meaning from the color name. "Cranberry" is a specific hue (#C50F1F) that reads as "danger red" but avoids the confusion of calling it "Red" when the brand might also use red for other purposes.

Why tint/shade numbered variants? Each status needs multiple intensity levels: Background1 (subtle container), Background2 (medium), Background3 (strong/filled). The numbered system makes it easy to pick the right intensity without manual hex tweaking. Light themes use tints (lighter) for backgrounds and shades (darker) for foregrounds; dark themes invert this.

Why Orange for Warning (not Yellow)? Fluent chose orange over yellow for the same accessibility reason Carbon encountered: pure yellow cannot achieve sufficient contrast on white at typical text sizes. Orange provides the "caution" association while maintaining readability. This is the same trade-off our DS makes.

3. Universal Patterns Across All Three Systems

Pattern 1: Grade/Tone Inversion Between Themes
All three systems use darker shades for light backgrounds and lighter shades for dark backgrounds. The specific color "identity" (hue) stays the same; only the lightness shifts. This is exactly what our DS does.

Pattern 2: Warning Yellow is Problematic
Pure yellow fails contrast on white at every useful lightness level. Carbon works around this by using yellow only as an icon color. Fluent and our DS both substitute orange. M3 leaves it to implementers.

Pattern 3: Never Rely on Color Alone
All three systems mandate pairing color with icons, text labels, or patterns. This is critical for color-blind users who may not distinguish red from green or orange from yellow.

Pattern 4: Container + On-Container Pairing
M3 pioneered this explicitly, but all systems use it: a tinted background (container) with dark text on it (on-container) for lower-emphasis status display. Our "tonal" emphasis level maps directly to this.

5. Hue Separation Analysis (Color-blind Safety)

For users with deuteranopia (red-green color blindness), the hue difference between danger and warning matters significantly. The recommended minimum separation is ~30 degrees, though lightness difference can compensate.

This "inverted" approach for warning mirrors M3's container pattern where the container is the prominent color and the text provides contrast.

6. Decisions Summary and Recommendations

Decision 1: Orange for Warning (Not Yellow) -- VALIDATED

Pure yellow cannot achieve AA (4.5:1) contrast on white at any useful lightness. Carbon works around this by restricting yellow to icon-only use. Fluent chose orange for the same reason. Our choice to use orange-amber for warning is the correct trade-off for a system where warning appears as buttons and text, not just icons.

Decision 2: Grade Inversion for Dark Mode -- VALIDATED

All three reference systems shift to lighter/brighter shades on dark backgrounds. Our approach of using brighter, desaturated tints in dark mode (e.g., danger goes from #d13029 to #ff8383) follows the universal pattern. Carbon shifts from Red 60 to Red 50; M3 shifts from Tone 40 to Tone 80. Same logic.

Decision 3: WideBot Warning Uses Inverted Contrast -- VALIDATED

WideBot's primary (orange) and warning (also orange family) needed differentiation. Using bright amber bg + dark text for warning (vs dark bg + white text for primary) mirrors M3's Container pattern and provides both color AND lightness differentiation. This is the strongest approach available.

Decision 4: Hulul Warning Hue Proximity -- ACCEPTABLE WITH MITIGATION

Hulul's 17 deg danger-warning separation is below the ideal 30 deg. However: Carbon's caution_major is also close at 28 deg. The critical mitigation (which our DS_RULES.md already mandates) is to never use color alone -- always pair with icons and text. Additionally, the tonal emphasis level shows the actual hue more clearly than filled, and the icon shapes (triangle-alert for warning, circle-x/trash for danger) provide a non-color signal.

Recommendation: Icon Pairing Mandate for Status Colors

Based on reference system analysis, add an explicit rule to DS_RULES.md: Status-colored components (buttons, alerts, badges, toasts) MUST pair the color with a semantically distinct icon. Recommended pairings: Danger = circle-x or trash, Warning = triangle-alert, Success = circle-check, Info = circle-info. This follows Carbon's status indicator pattern and provides color-blind safety across all brands.
